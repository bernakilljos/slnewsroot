RECOMMENDATIONS 
 
 
1. The Task and Finish Group recommends the following to Cabinet:  
 
Recommendation One 
 
There be consideration of additional resource being established to assist with 
analysing data gathered during stock condition surveys. It is considered that having 
a clear picture and analysis of the stock condition data will enable senior leaders 
and members to prioritise investment based on resident safety and impact. 
 
Recommendation Two 
 
Improve the resident repair journey by reducing appointment windows and including 
a digital / online tracking element so that residents can track the raising of their 
repair order, view their appointment slot, track lateness and reschedule their 
appointment online if necessary as well as provide feedback on the service 
immediately after. 
 
Recommendation Three 
 
Co-design the repairs and maintenance contract specifications with residents and 
communicate with tenants how tenants’ voice has influenced service design. 
 
Recommendation Four 
 
Use resident provided feedback and data in robust monitoring of repairs and 
maintenance contracts. 
 
Recommendation Five 
 
The Borough Residents’ Forum constitution and future meeting dates should 
therefore be revised and updated to reflect the change in the Council’s governance. 
 
Recommendation Six 
 
Tenant and leaseholder representation be included in the governance 
arrangements monitoring performance and delivery of improvements to the 
Council’s managed housing stock. 
 
Recommendation Seven 
 
Commission specialist external consultants on the engagement strategy, to include 
both the formal engagement structures including Residents Associations, and 
informal engagement processes, with a view to concluding their review by June 
2026.

INTRODUCTION 
 
3. At the 18th June 2025 Housing Overview and Scrutiny Committee, Members 
requested that a task and finish group be established in response to the C3 grading 
by the Regulator of Social Housing following their inspection in late 2024 and the 
publication of their findings in February 2025. Members wanted to ensure that 
improvements were made, and measures were put in place so that all consumer 
standards were met and council tenants were provided with the highest quality 
service, with a view to ensuring a higher grade is achieved when the Council is re-
inspected. 
 
4. The Social Housing Improvement Task and Finish Group was subsequently 
established, with the membership comprising Councillors Stock (Chair), Ayres, 
Davies, Govindia and Mrs Graham. The Group also agreed to invite residents Tom 
Glockner, Vice-Chair of the Borough Residents’ Forum (BRF) and Joanna Playford, 
Tenant representative of the Association of Retained Council Housing (ARCH) to 
their November and December meetings. 
 
5. The Group first met on 22nd September and agreed their objectives to monitor and 
scrutinise improvement actions in relation to the Regulator of Social Housing 
Inspection Report and the Council’s engagement with the Housing Ombudsman 
and the Building Safety Regulator as well as feedback received through resident 
engagement structures, with a view to gaining assurance that the Council will be in 
a position to achieve a higher grading when re-inspected, and to ensure that the 
changes are sustainable and enduring, so tenants are always provided the highest 
quality of services. 
 
6. Members agreed that the areas for consideration as part of the review would involve 
scrutiny of the adequacy and ambition of the Housing Department’s Improvement 
Plan in response to the C3 grading. The review would also include identifying 
measures and milestones towards the achievement of a higher grading, with a 
focus on: 
 
• Plans for stock condition surveys 
• The performance of the electrical safety check programme  
• Performance in relation to fire remedial actions 
• Plans for informal residential engagement 
• Tracking performance in relation to repairs  

5 
 
 
7. The Group hoped to contribute to the commitment to achieving the best possible 
rating but at the very least achieving a C2 grading, noting that the timings of any 
reassessment and/or re-inspection are at the discretion of the regulator. The 
Group’s role in achieving this outcome will be through the scrutiny of improvement 
actions and in making recommendations as determined through the course of the 
review.

9. In addition to the regular Task and Finish Group meetings, the Task and Finish 
Group were invited to attend the Residents’ Conference on 15th October. The Chair 
attended the Borough Residents Forum to present an interim report on 20th 
November, and an estate walkabout on 3rd December on the Eastwood South 
estate. The Chair, along with officers, also attended a Parent Champions’ meeting 
on 12th December at the Training Resource Centre to gather feedback from tenants 
to inform the Task and Finish Group’s report into the plan for improvements to 
social housing following the Regulator of Social Housing’s report. 
 
 
FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
 
10. Below is a summary of some of the key matters and recommendations considered 
by the Task and Finish Group, with updated figures and data as of 9th January. 
 
 
Improvement Tracker 
 
11. The Housing Improvement Plan was not ready to be shared with the Group during 
the course of the review. The reasons for this were explained to the Group and it 
was noted that there had been a Housing Improvement Programme Board in place 
to steer its development. With the establishment of the Resident Services 
department on 1st October 2025, Members were advised that officers had needed 
more time to work on the wider plan, including displaying the priorities within it, 
developing a RAG status, and ensuring appropriate funding for those actions. The 
format of the plan also needed to be first confirmed with the Social Housing 
Regulator.   
 
12. It was noted that the Housing Improvement Plan was due to be considered by the 
Cabinet at its meeting of 26th January 2026. The Task and Finish Group noted their 
aspiration for the recommendations they made to be reflected in the Housing 
Improvement Plan. 
 
13. There had been discussions in relation to resident involvement in the development 
of the Improvement Plan, but it was noted that due to time constraints this had not 
been possible on this occasion. However, the Task and Finish Group had sought 
assurance that with stronger resident engagement more generally in future, that 
tenants’ and leaseholders’ voices and feedback would shape future iterations and 
versions of the Improvement Plan. 
 
14. Despite there not being an agreed Housing Improvement Plan, Members were 
assured that improvement works had still been ongoing and were presented with an 
Improvement Tracker at their November and December meetings. It was noted that 
the Improvement Tracker was provided for the Social Housing Regulator on a 
monthly basis. It was noted that the Social Housing Regulator’s comments on the 
Improvement Tracker had so far been positive overall. 
 
 
 
 

8 
 
Stock Condition Surveys 
 
14. Members were provided with an explanation of how the Stock Condition surveys 
were undertaken and it was reported that the department was performing well in 
terms of undertaking the stock condition surveys in relation to its commitment to 
survey 100% of tenanted homes. 2,540 property surveys and 1,320 block surveys 
had been undertaken by the end of 2025, with the department having exceeded its 
goal of 2,900 by the end of the year. 
 
15. The Group was advised that the initial target had been to inspect all blocks within 
five years so the Council could provide assurance on the decency of its homes but 
had recently been revised to achieve 100% of surveys by the end of 2026.  The 
Group endorsed the revised timescale which recognises the urgency required to 
effectively prioritise maintenance and inform investment in the Council’s housing 
stock.  
 
16. Members heard that a large amount of data was being generated from those 
surveys and analysing that volume of information was a challenge. It was noted that 
robust data is critical and analysing stock condition information would help inform 
the major works programme. The potential benefit for additional resource being 
established to assist with analysing the data gathered during the surveys was 
discussed, with it being considered that it would enable the department to properly 
and swiftly analyse the range of data, where blocks with particular investment needs 
could be proactively identified.  
 
Recommendation One 
 
There be consideration of additional resource being established to assist with 
analysing data gathered during stock condition surveys. It is considered that 
having a clear picture and analysis of the stock condition data will enable senior 
leaders and Members to prioritise investment based on resident safety and 
impact.  
 
 
17. In response to block issues identified so far through stock condition surveys, the 
Group was advised that Category 1 hazards predominantly related to mould growth 
and missing CO and smoke alarms. This was also linked to resident vulnerabilities 
and working out where residents were not likely to report issues. For example, it 
was known that some residents had not reported certain issues such as damp and 
mould.  The Group acknowledged that acceleration of the stock condition surveys 
may result in the identification of Category 1 hazards and recognised the 
importance of timely action and robust monitoring of the department’s response to 
the hazards identified.   
 
Performance of the electrical safety check programme 
 
18. The Group have been provided with updates regarding the progress in completing 
electrical safety checks. It was noted that significant improvements had been made 
on completing electrical safety certificates, with it being confirmed that there was 
only 1 more communal block to test, although that would be more challenging as 
there was a requirement to ensure all residents are in at the time of the testing. 

9 
 
19. However, there were some access issues over the remaining properties to inspect. 
The process in relation to gaining access to properties for electrical testing was 
described to the Group, which involved an appointment letter being initially sent to 
the resident by the contractor. The Council would then also try to make contact if 
the resident did not respond to the appointment letter, and a final warning letter 
would then be issued and if necessary, legal action in the form of an injunction 
would be requested from the legal team. Members were advised that phone calls to 
residents were also made.  
 
20. The steps as set out in the process had to be followed as in order to apply for an 
injunction if it was ultimately required, the Council would need to demonstrate that 
every other possible step had been exhausted. This would include providing copies 
of dated letters sent to the residents. It was noted that there were 191 cases where 
access could not be gained into those properties. 
 
21. However, it was considered that from a tenant’s perspective, a significant element 
of their issues related to contractor communication. For example, a resident may 
not have received the original appointment letter, so alarm could be caused when 
they received further letters requesting for contact to be made and noting the 
potential for legal action. It was suggested that the Council could work with Resident 
Associations to try and get them to assist. 
 
22. Tenants noted difficulties in accommodating appointment times for safety checks 
and stock condition surveys because of work or family commitments. Tenants 
mentioned that contractors sometimes failed to show up for scheduled 
appointments which led to frustration.  Members heard that officers were exploring 
whether multiple checks could be undertaken in a single visit. The Group agreed 
that this warranted further exploration as well as a department wide approach of 
making every contact count to gather information about the condition of homes and 
residents needs alongside ensuring that robust data systems are in place to record 
the information obtained.  
 
23. The Group considered there was merit in promoting the reasons for the Council 
needing to gain access into properties as part of roadshow events and it was noted 
there was a commitment for a programme of roadshows next year, which could be 
used to better co-ordinate explanations for the health and safety benefits to 
residents allowing access to their properties for the necessary checks. 
 
Performance in relation to fire remedial actions 
 
24. Updates were provided on the work being undertaken in relation to Fire Risk 
Assessments (FRAs). Where actions had become overdue, Estate Services officers 
were carrying out bi-weekly inspections to ensure that communal areas and intake 
cupboards were free from combustible materials. In parallel, they were reviewing 
blocks to identify and address any other fire safety concerns within the communal 
areas. Members were advised there were new FRAs for high-rise buildings, which 
were being worked through with an aim for completion before April 2026. It was 
reported that as well as needing to complete the overdue FRAs reported by the 
regulator, additional new FRAs were being received. Officers were working on 
completing both the overdue actions and the new, incoming actions, but it was 
noted that there would eventually be a split in the data presented to the SHR, 
between older, overdue actions and new ones, so that it would be clear as to which 
they were.  

10 
 
 
25. Members were advised about the involvement of the London Fire Brigade (LFB) in 
regard to FRAs and it was confirmed that officers met regularly with the Borough 
Commander and the London Fire Brigade (LFB). However, the Council had a 
Building Safety Lead officer who was responsible for managing and signing off 
FRAs. 
 
26. Members heard that the target for completion of serious actions was three months 
from the date of the Fire Risk Assessments. For those that were overdue, most 
were in progress which usually meant that a repair order had been raised, and that 
a contractor would be carrying out the work in the coming weeks. 
 
Repairs  
 
27. The Group have been provided with information in relation to the Council’s repairs 
services. Repairs are central to a tenant’s experience of the housing service so it is 
right that the Council’s corporate plan is ambitious in its commitment that the 
Council “will ensure accountability on the Council’s Housing repairs and cleaning, 
and all will be done promptly and to a high standard.” 
 
28. 80,000 repairs were completed last year with the majority being delivered by 
external contractors as part of the Area Repair Contracts (ARCs). The ARCs started 
in 2021 and end in September 2026. The Group were informed that a full review of 
the repairs process is underway, and the review has been informed by a resident 
focus group on repairs.   
 
29. The Group was informed that there is an opportunity to use technology to improve 
repairs handling, for example with Netcall 2 coming in, there was the potential to 
take video calls with residents to look at repairs, rather than officers having to 
physically visit properties in all instances. This was seen to be providing residents 
with more choice, and this would initially be a pilot, to gain the trust of residents and 
to see how well it works.  
 
30. Resident feedback on repairs includes delays in addressing repairs, repeated visits 
for the same problem, and contractors failing to complete jobs or providing 
adequate solutions. For example, some residents reported that contractors 
repeatedly patched leaks instead of addressing the root cause, leading to ongoing 
issues.  This feedback reflects the Tenant Satisfaction Measure for satisfaction with 
repairs which was relatively low at 58% in 2024/25, an increase of 4% from 
2023/24.  
 
31. The Group was informed that the repairs review is considering the processes used 
by residents to report and track their repairs. Residents highlighted issues with the 
Council’s online repair portal, noting that it lacked sufficient options to accurately 
report problems and suggested that features such as the ability to upload photos 
and receive real-time updates on repair progress would be beneficial.  There was a 
request for better tracking and transparency in the repair process, with residents 
requesting a system that showed the progress of their reported issues, including 
who was handling their case and expected timelines. The Group heard that the 
Council has sought to learn from other landlords and has conducted visits to other 
councils, including Camden. The Group endorsed transforming the resident repair 

11 
 
journey to ensure it is customer focussed, including providing greater opportunities 
to report and track repairs online in real time, similar to modern parcel delivery 
tracking systems. Members endorsed the plans to expand the use of online systems 
and were assured that the Council was committed to accessibility, ensuring that a 
non-digital option would always be available. 
 
Recommendation Two 
 
Improve the resident repair journey by reducing appointment windows and 
including a digital / online tracking element so that residents can track the raising 
of their repair order, view their appointment slot, track lateness and reschedule 
their appointment online if necessary as well as provide feedback on the service 
immediately after. 
 
 
32. The Group discussed that the re-procurement of the repairs contracts presents an 
opportunity for tenants’ voices to influence service design and for tenants to be 
meaningfully involved in decision making by co-designing the service. There was 
also an opportunity to strengthen resident involvement in robust contract monitoring 
to ensure a customer focussed approach to repairs handling which prioritises the 
quality of the tenant experience.  
 
Recommendation Three 
 
Co-design the repairs and maintenance contract specifications with residents and 
communicate with tenants how tenants’ voice has influenced service design.  
 
 
 
Recommendation Four 
 
Use resident provided feedback and data in robust monitoring of repairs and 
maintenance contracts.  
 
 
Resident Engagement 
 
33. The task and finish group received some initial feedback from the Residents’ 
Conference, which was held on 15th October with around 110 residents attending in 
total. Presentations at the conference did not just focus on responding to the Social 
Housing Regulator, but also in looking at improvements in other areas, such as a 
presentation on ‘Empowering Residents’ from the Resident Participation Manager, 
which was a newly created role where residents were asked for feedback on what 
housing services they would like to shape and how. Members were advised that the 
Conference had been really focused on workshop sessions and officers had been 
left with a large list of actions following good workshop discussions on topics such 
as clubrooms and gardens, as well as one on informal engagement. 
 

12 
 
34. The existing formal resident engagement structures were set out for the Task and 
Finish Group. There were 38 Resident Associations (with ongoing work to increase 
these in number), 4 Area Housing Panels, Co-op Forums, and the Borough 
Residents’ Forum. The feedback from the Social Housing Regulator had been that 
this was a well-established formal engagement framework overall. However, 
Members noted that consideration was required into how the Borough Residents’ 
Forum fit into the Council’s new decision-making model, as Cabinet papers were no 
longer required to first be considered at the Housing Overview and Scrutiny 
Committee. The Borough Residents’ Forum constitution and future meeting dates 
should therefore be revised and updated to reflect the change in the Council’s 
governance. 
 
Recommendation Five 
 
The Borough Residents’ Forum constitution and future meeting dates should 
therefore be revised and updated to reflect the change in the Council’s 
governance. 
 
 
35. The Group discussed a cultural shift that places residents first and people at the 
heart of decision making. This involves embedding a culture that recognises 
residents as experts in their own lived experience and reframing the Council’s role - 
not as a landlord managing tenants but a partner alongside tenants working 
collaboratively together.  During the review the Task and Finish Group has been 
joined by and met with residents who have shared valuable insights. It is 
acknowledged that incorporating resident voices into governance structures, such 
as Area Housing Panels and the Borough Residents’ Forum brings substantial 
benefits. It is considered that there is value in residents continuing to play an active 
role in scrutinising the performance and monitoring delivery of the improvement 
plan.   
 
 
Recommendation Six 
 
Tennant and leaseholder representation be included in the governance 
arrangements monitoring performance and delivery of improvements to the 
Council’s managed housing stock.  
 
 
36. Resident Associations only covered around one third of the Council’s housing stock, 
and there were a large number of estates with no Resident Associations. There was 
an aim to widen the group of residents so that there were more opportunities for 
service delivery contributions. They tended to be more leaseholder dominated, 
which had also meant that the Residents’ Conference had been dominated to an 
extent with leasehold issues. It was noted that the feedback from the SHR referred 
to limited tenant-led engagement, as well as limited engagement opportunities for 
Resident Association members, weaknesses in tenant influence on decision making 
and tenant influence being poorly communicated.  
 
 
 

13 
 
 
37. The informal engagement processes around the time of the Social Housing 
Regulator inspection included Housing Community Champions, estate inspections 
led by Area Housing Managers which involved residents, focus groups, and a 
participation register with around 100 residents (not necessarily all from Resident 
Associations) registered following the Residents’ Conference.  
 
38. Since then, work had been done to increase the number of residents on the 
participation register to over 700, with over 500 tenants. Members were informed 
that focus groups had been held earlier in the year on repairs and complaints, as 
well as one on Resident Association accreditation. There had been four trial 
Housing Roadshows held in September, with over 200 residents in attendance. The 
roadshows had helped to engage more face-to-face with residents and increase 
visibility of a number of different Council teams. 
 
39. Other areas of informal engagement were highlighted and included Readers 
Panels, where residents worked with council officers to redesign the performance 
information shared with the Area Housing Panels (these will be used from Q3 
2025/26 onwards). Readers Panels have also been used to check the accessibility 
of easy-read versions of key policies. Another area of informal engagement 
included online surveys for new tenant welcome packs which would provide a better 
start for tenants in their new homes, as well as a Domestic Abuse policy being co-
developed with survivors of domestic abuse. 
 
40. The next steps for informal engagement were set out and included a calendar of 
engagement events for 2026, a quarterly Participation Register newsletter to raise 
awareness of upcoming events amongst residents, as well as enabling residents to 
see any outcomes following the focus groups they have been involved in. Since the 
establishment of the Resident Services department, it was noted that further 
communications would need to be done, such as more internal promotion within the 
Council as well as some external tenant engagement specialists being 
commissioned to review the changes made. 
 
Recommendation Seven 
 
Commission specialist external consultants on the engagement strategy, to 
include both the formal engagement structures including Residents Associations, 
and informal engagement processes, with a view to concluding their review by 
June 2026. 
 
 
41. Members also considered the 2025 Tenant Satisfaction Measures. Overall tenant 
satisfaction had slightly improved on the previous year’s results, increasing to 65% 
this year from 64% in 2024. The areas where tenants were most satisfied included 
satisfaction that their homes are safe (70%) and that the Council treated tenants 
with fairness and respect (78%). Overall, there was improvement across seven of 
the 12 Tenant Perception measures compared. 
 
 
 

14 
 
42. In 2024/25 62% of tenants were satisfied that the Council kept tenants informed 
about things that matter to them.  During the course of the review residents 
identified a broader need to improve communication including responsiveness, 
simplicity and clarity.  Tenants confirmed that they received Homelife newsletter but 
did not necessarily retain copies to refer back to, addressed letters were more likely 
to be read and greater use of SMS text messages in the future was welcomed.  The 
Group considered that work should be done to improve communication standards.  
 
43. The current process for data collection was explained, which involved one big 
satisfaction survey undertaken in June/July that was submitted to the Social 
Housing Regulator annually. However, from next year this would be undertaken on 
a monthly basis. The annual submission to the Social Housing regulator still needed 
1,100 tenants to be surveyed each year, which was a set target. Moving to a 
monthly basis would involve surveying around 90 households per month. The data 
over the 12-month period would then be sent to the Social Housing Regulator. It 
was noted this change would provide a more real time picture of tenants’ 
perceptions of the service. Members also noted that the consultants responsible for 
the surveys could tailor them to any format required and ensure that all surveys 
were representative so that the overall sample size was representative of the 
borough’s demographics. 
 
44. Members noted that in the past the Tenant Satisfaction Measures had been 
presented to the Housing Overview and Scrutiny Committee, and whilst they had 
not been selected by Committee Members in their work programming this year, the 
Task and Finish Group considered that they should be presented on an annual 
basis, as including them on an agenda and discussing the results in a public 
meeting would help to better highlight tenants’ perceptions of the service.  
 
Recommendation Eight 
 
The Tenant Satisfaction Measures be presented to the Housing Overview and 
Scrutiny Committee on an annual basis. 
 
 
 
Customer care and Complaints 
 
45. The statistics for complaints were considered by the Task and Finish Group. The 
upheld or partially upheld complaints at 64% were considered by Members to be a 
significantly high percentage, but the Group was advised this reflected a change in 
the way that complaints were dealt with, as well as the change in the approach from 
the Housing Ombudsman. An expression of dissatisfaction would now be logged as 
a stage 1 complaint, with a view that all complaints were now considered to be 
opportunities to learn, which would gradually help drive improvements for the 
service overall. It was noted that presentations at the ARCH conference had 
demonstrated that housing departments with an increase in complaints often 
correlated well with increased overall satisfaction. 
 
 
 
 

15 
 
 
46. Members were also advised that it was quite common for residents to complain 
about a number of different issues at one time, and there could be a case that the 
department had upheld one of those issues raised. For example, it could be 
concluded that all of the repair issues were dealt with adequately as requests for 
service, but the initial correspondence contained a complaint at that earlier date 
which was not treated at Stage 1 of the complaint’s procedure. It was added that if 
complaints were not partially upheld at that stage, the Housing Ombudsman would 
find maladministration when they investigated and that the Council were being far 
more robust in recognising where learning would need to be made. 
 
47. In relation to vulnerable residents, Members heard that the department was working 
with other departments to share data on vulnerable residents where appropriate. 
Notably, there was a piece of work being undertaken in liaison with colleagues in 
Children’s and Adults Services to ensure that all teams had the same accessible 
data on vulnerable residents. Residents would be approached directly to explain 
their vulnerabilities and also those of other people in their households. Languages 
as well as any other adjustments would be factored in, and residents would be 
asked how they would prefer to be contacted. 
 
 
Housing Ombudsman and Building Safety Regulator 
 
48. An update was provided to the task and finish group on the Housing Ombudsman’s 
investigation. It was explained that the Housing Ombudsman had asked for a 
lengthy list of evidence, for example internal working procedures, for which the 
Council had been given a deadline of 29th November to provide the requested 
information. That evidence and material was submitted and is in the process of 
being reviewed by the Housing Ombudsman. It is not expected that the Council 
would hear back until later in 2026.  
 
49. Likewise, an update was also provided on the current position with the Building 
Safety Regulator. Members were advised that on a number of cases the BSR has 
requested further evidence be provided to aid their assessment. 
 
50. It was therefore not possible for the task and finish group to have considered any 
findings of improvement actions from the Housing Ombudsman and Building Safety 
Regulator, as was initially envisaged. 
 
 
CONCLUDING REMARKS 
  
51. The Group thanks officers who have supported the review and residents who have 
shared their insights during the course of the review. This report summarises what 
Members have considered throughout the review and presents their 
recommendations. Subject to approval by the Housing Overview and Scrutiny 
Committee, the report will be presented to the Cabinet for their consideration of the 
recommendations. 
 
 

16 
 
52. The progress and work undertaken to date, including noting the wider Improvement 
Plan was due to be considered by Cabinet at its January meeting, was 
acknowledged. However, whilst noting that the timings of any reassessment and/or 
re-inspection were at the discretion of the regulator, the Task and Finish Group 
recognised the importance of openness and transparency and considered that the 
improvement journey still had some way to go before the Council was able to meet 
the commitment to achieving the best possible rating but at the very least achieving 
a C2 grading. Therefore, whilst the review was concluding for now before the pre-
election period, Members considered that the matter remain as a priority inclusion 
for the Housing Overview and Scrutiny Committee’s work programme post-election. 
 
Recommendation Nine 
 
The Housing Overview and Scrutiny Committee continue to scrutinise the matter 
and oversee progress and include it on their work programme post-election.
