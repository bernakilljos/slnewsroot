Independent Review of Wandsworth Council’s response 
to the Fox House fire, Maysoule Road, Battersea 
on Wednesday 10 September 2025 
report dated: 31 December 2025 
Report Author 
Barry Quirk CBE PhD BSc FRGS 
1

The background and context 
1.Fox House is a post-war five storey block of 37 ‘general needs’ flats.  It is a brick 
constructed property with a pitched roof.  It is one of a series of similar blocks at 
Maysoule Road in Battersea.  At the time of the fire all but one of the properties were 
occupied - one was being refurbished by its leasehold owner.  There were over 100 
occupants living in the block: mostly tenants of the Council (29), some leaseholders 
(five, including the property being refurbished) as well as others three households living 
there on a temporary licence, while awaiting their permanent rehousing. 
2.A multi-generational and diverse community of people lived in the block.  Single adults 
and couples who had lived for decades, and other families with young and growing 
children who had moved to the block more recently.  This was a thriving small 
community of people with strong attachments to their local area and yet also with wider 
links to London’s economy, public services and educational institutions.   
3.Wednesday 10th September 2025 was a cool day in Wandsworth with intermittent light 
rain. The day was darkening when the fire ignited at Fox House and spread quickly 
across the roof of the block.  Some residents first saw the fire above them reflecting in 
the windows of the block opposite.  One described the horror of watching this reflection, 
when the full window above their flat was blown out and crash landed on the ground 
immediately below.  Neighbours began to alert each other as to what was happening.  It 
was clear to them that they should all get out as quickly as possible.  The balcony 
walkways enabled a quick self-organised evacuation.  This swift neighbourly action was 
crucial in minimising harm to all who lived there.   
4.The London Fire Brigade arrived rapidly at sunset (about 7.20pm).  And while the cause 
of the fire was not clear, the cause of the spread of the fire was obvious.  The fire had 
spread across the roof space in around ten minutes - a space that is several metres wide 
and more than 50 metres long.                                   (photo, London Fire Brigade, BBC news)   
2


5.LFB’s report of the incident explains how fifteen fire engines and around 100 
firefighters were called to the scene on Maysoule Road in Battersea.   
“The fourth floor and the roof space of a five-storey residential building were 
destroyed by fire.  Around 150 people (their then estimate) were evacuated from the 
building by fire crews wearing breathing apparatus, with around 60 people 
displaced. There were no reports of any injuries.  Two of the Brigade's 32-metre 
turntable ladders were used as water towers to help extinguish the fire from height. 
The Brigade's drone team also attended, providing the Incident Commander with a 
greater situational awareness of the fire.  The Brigade took the first of 42 calls at 
1909 and mobilised crews from Battersea, Wandsworth, Fulham, Clapham and other 
surrounding fire stations to the scene. The fire was brought under control at 2301.”  
6.One resident reported to the BBC that, “I opened my door and my neighbour said ‘fire, 
fire upstairs’. I took my phone which was near me, my house keys, my jacket and I 
forgot my glasses and I ran downstairs.  I crossed over with firemen on the way down 
and then I came out and I saw the fire coming out of the window really violently.”   
(photo: London Fire Brigade, BBC news) 
7.The existing Fire Risk Assessment (FRA) for the block was completed by independent 
experts in 2022.  The roof void was not inspected.  The recommended safety guidance, 
for residents in the event of a fire was to ‘stay put’.  Given the experience of this 
incident at Fox House and the relative ease by which most residents were able to 
evacuate from this walk-up block of four storeys, the implications of these two points 
may need to be explored further and consideration given to whether FRAs and safety 
advice to residents need reviewing. No residents were known to have a PEEP - a 
personal emergency evacuation plan.  This is a bespoke plan created for individuals who 
may need assistance or have specific requirements to evacuate a building (a workplace, 
care home or residential building) safely during an emergency. 
3


8.An early contact to the Council was from the LFB to the duty officer in building control.  
Their expertise in building engineering, chartered surveying and especially with 
assessing dangerous structures, means that LFB have a regular connection with building 
control on fire related issues with buildings of all types.  In this instance, the concern 
was not simply about fire damage and the impact on the building of water penetration, 
but also the apparent fragility of chimney stacks and the positioning of electrical cables.  
Building control were at the site by 11pm and in discussion with LFB. The structure was 
declared unsafe at the scene.  Scaffolding and fencing had to be erected quickly for 
purposes of public safety - and the formal dangerous structure notice was issued two 
days later. 
9.Imagine this: you realise, with an immense sense of fear, that there’s a raging fire in 
your roof space.  You are desperate to flee.  A mixture of adrenaline and common sense 
compels you to leave as rapidly as you can.  But you immediately think, “what do I need 
to take with me now; what if the whole place is consumed by flames and I lose 
everything?” My phone, my pet, my purse or wallet - but what about my key identity 
documents, my glasses, hearing aids, and essential medicines.  And what about my 
treasured personal and family mementos.  How long do I have? What shall I leave 
behind?  Everyone who fled Fox House that night had to make this calculation.  They 
were safe - but some of their treasured belongings were lost.  
The Council’s emergency and civil contingency responsibilities 
10.Local authorities are central to disaster management and emergency response, acting as 
key coordinators and providers of aid within their communities through multi-agency 
partnerships known as Local Resilience Forums (LRF).  Their roles include managing 
evacuation, providing social support, liaising with other emergency services, and 
coordinating the long-term recovery process.  The Civil Contingencies Act of 2004 
designates Councils as “Category 1 Responders” and they are expected to be prepared 
for emergencies outside of normal working hours.  
11.These responsibilities, as a first responder in civil contingencies, apply to the Council as 
a corporate body and not simply to one or several functions of the Council.  As a 
housing authority and residential building owner, the Council is responsible as a 
landlord to its tenants and leaseholders.  If a boiler breaks down or pipes freeze through, 
the Council responds through its housing function as a landlord.  Its housing 
management division holds the key responsibility for resolving these building problems.   
12.But if over 100 people need to be evacuated from their homes on a long term temporary 
or permanent basis, this is plainly a corporate responsibility for the Council overall. Of 
course, the housing function will be centrally involved in any emergency that involves 
its own housing stock, but it is the Council as a corporate emergency responder which 
holds prime responsibility for major incidents.  Consider for a moment, the response if 
this incident occurred in a housing association block or in a private residential block.  
4

13.In Wandsworth the housing function has taken the lead for responding to emergencies 
for many years.  This was a pragmatic and sensible approach prior to the 2004 Civil 
Contingencies Act, as the housing department had some 30,000 homes under 
management and thereby had considerable experience in dealing with emergency call-
outs, organising temporary shelter, and using estate based assets as community rest 
centres.  This contrasts with the position in Richmond, where the transfer of just over 
7,000 homes to a housing partnership in the year 2000, meant that adult social care 
function took the lead on emergency response, in the light of its access, in the case of 
localised emergencies, to local day care centres.   
14.It is important that both Richmond and Wandsworth have the best possible arrangements 
for responding to emergencies and that the single staff organisation is both robust and 
flexible in the delivery of responses, in both authorities, to emergencies and in aiding 
subsequent recovery.  The two geographies of the two Councils may present a slightly 
different pattern of risks but the organisational response needs to be consistently 
effective, not least because of the scale capabilities that can be drawn upon.   
15.Councils often view their primary role as improving service and project delivery.  But in 
truth, community safety, civil resilience and the protection of the public from extreme 
risks, hazards and harm is a first order requirement on all Councils.   
16.The conventional approach for local authorities, in respect of organising themselves for 
responding to emergencies, involves the development of disciplined emergency and civil 
contingency plans embedded within wide ranging risk management strategies. To this 
end, emergency planning teams are corporately engaged on preparedness planning, 
scenario testing, and ensuring that the whole organisation is suitably trained to be 
mobilised and deployed in the event of a large scale emergency.   
17.These teams also develop an arrangement for strategic, tactical and operational 
responses through a classic “gold, silver and bronze” framework of control, 
communication and empowered delegation.  The orthodox operating framework for 
London authorities requires the LALO to have command and control over resources at 
the scene.  In this way the LALO essentially fulfils a ‘bronze commander’ role, while 
also representing the Silver at on-scene tactical co-ordination group meetings.  
18.It was in this context that Richmond and Wandsworth’s chief executive established a 
new approach to allocating responsibilities to the emergency gold and silver roles only 
two months before the Fox House fire incident.  His aim was to build upon the 
traditional structures of emergency response by establishing a more corporate response 
from management across the organisation.  The emergency planning team had only 
recently set a new rota of responsibilities involving a wider range of senior managers 
than had previously been the case.  This approach was in its infancy and was tested and 
strained by the requirements to respond adequately in the case of the Fox House fire.    
5

Planning is crucial, readiness to respond is vital 
19.When an emergency occurs, it’s important to refer to the plan - how people and 
resources are to be organised and deployed, and how communication is to be channelled 
and disseminated.  But actions must be guided by the specific circumstances of the 
emergency itself; its immediate consequences, its likely spread, in space and time, and 
its immediate and short-term demands upon the organisation.  The original national 
guidance produced for emergency response was drafted before the iPhone was 
developed.  Of course, it has been updated and revised but the dynamic social media 
environment proves very challenging in the practice of emergency management in 2025. 
20.Video accounts of the Fox House fire and its consequences were available on social 
media within minutes of the incident starting.  Viral spread of video based social media 
information is the environment in which emergencies now have to be managed.  
Cascading telephone calls between a hierarchy of officers, most of whom are not 
actually at the scene, simply won’t work adequately in 2025.  Events of this scale - with 
over 100 people evacuated - requires senior managers capable of strategically assessing 
the people and resources that need to be mobilised immediately; certainly within the 
first three hours of an incident.  In their target response time to a fire, LFB aim to be at 
the scene within six minutes (with a second fire engine arriving within two minutes of 
the first).  In the field of tackling crime, the police speak of the first golden hour for a 
successful investigation.  In the event of a community emergency, Councils need to 
consider the commensurate urgency of their response - as the table suggests below, a 
response within two hours ought to be the outer limit for setting a strategic response.

The initial response 
21.The Council’s emergency arrangements were stood up at around 7.50.  The borough 
emergency centre (BEC) wasn’t set up, but the joint control centre (JCC) and the local 
authority LALO was on notice and communicating from 8pm.  Measured against its 
6

own expectations the organisation’s response was adequate and satisfactory.  A rest 
centre was established in the community hall adjacent to the York Gardens Library.  A 
gold (chief executive), silver (a director) and an operational service head acting as a 
local authority liaison officer (or LALO) were alerted within expected times and the 
area housing manager was present at the scene by 8pm.  
22.From this time the chief executive, as Gold, had been advised of the incident and was in 
regular contact with both the Council’s political leadership and with the officer fulfilling 
the off-site Silver role.  This enabled the Council’s leading Members and executive 
managers to steer the implementation of the response and begin to guide the approach to 
recovery (especially in terms of rehousing).  A regular written briefing from the 
Council’s communications established an effective communication channel for relevant 
Members (including the local MP) as well as with relevant officers across the authority.     
23.In this incident, the Met Police were onsite before Council staff, and so they set up the 
reception centre - first temporarily in the adjacent Battersea Sports Centre; and then at 
around 9.30pm it was transferred to the York Gardens Library, by which time the Area 
Housing Manager had been onsite for some while and was principally engaged sorting 
hotel accommodation.  An experienced LALO arrived at 9.15pm - he had alerted 
operational staff (carpenters, depot staff, etc) to be on stand by while travelling to the 
site.  Both the LFB and the Met Police responded positively about the Council’s initial 
response and its liaison with them.  They both consider that it worked effectively. 
24.However, of the more than 100 residents who had evacuated, about two-thirds could not 
stay overnight with family or friends and required the Council’s support.  And as the 
darkness fell and residents thoughts were shifting towards “where are we sleeping 
tonight”, time began to collapse inwards for the officers on the scene.  The challenges of 
arranging urgent hotel accommodation worsened when it was evident that the 
organisation that was relied upon to deliver was unable to act as rapidly as required.  
ICAB - the insurance claims accommodation bureaux - found here, icab.org.uk - are 
specialists in arranging both planned and emergency accommodation.  They act on 
behalf of insurers, social housing organisations and a large range of Instructing 
Principals.  The Area Housing Manager recalled that, on the night, it took over an hour 
to house one family in a hotel, at which point the scale of the task involved became 
tangibly evident.  
25.This problem was compounded by two constraints; one extraneous, the other caused by 
internal bureaucracy.  The first, was that the tube strike in London had resulted in more 
commuters staying in the sort of modestly priced hotels usually used by ICAB. The 
second was that a pre-set £15 taxi voucher limit had the effect of constraining the 
distance that people could be taken to hotels.  It soon became evident to the officers 
struggling with the rehousing that they had to work around these constraints.   
7

26.Two councillors were present at the York Gardens Library and quickly found a highly 
valued pastoral role, supporting individuals and families. They soon realised that they 
were the only women present from the statutory authorities.  This is a powerful 
observation.  It was suggested, and without a scintilla of criticism for the dedicated work 
of all of the officers present, as well as those from the police and the fire service, that a 
slightly more balanced workforce might have been helpful on the night.  For more than 
half of the evacuated residents in the support centre were women, and children, and yet 
almost all the staff responding on the ground were men.  The councillor I spoke with 
stayed with residents in the support centre until 5am the next morning, when the last 
resident left to go to a hotel. 
27.There is no question about the sheer hard work and the personal responsibility grasped 
by those officers on the ground that evening.  They were doing their best and making 
their best judgements - against the odds of not being able to find hotels that night.  
However, there was a break in the communication chain between those officers on the 
ground and those who were responsible for the overall response.  This can be evidenced 
in a number of examples: the emergency planner was only able to speak just once to the 
officer dealing with the overnight stay problem; and the director who was acting as 
silver was told at 1am that everyone was allocated a hotel room at 1am, and then some 
45 minutes later was advised that around 40 people were still in the rest centre and had 
nowhere to go.  There is also little doubt that middle and senior managers stepped up to 
try to resolve these problems.  Some worked throughout the night, and others were still 
driving families to hotels in Wembley and Teddington in the early hours of the morning. 
28.But for many residents it was a shaky start.  They did not receive sufficiently reliable 
and timely information through the evening and the night.  They had left their homes in 
a hurry, and simple things had understandably been left behind - such as a change of 
clothing or mobile phone chargers.  Indeed, everyone’s mobile phone seemed to be 
dying at the same time; the evacuated residents’ as well as the mobiles of some of the 
staff who went to assist.  The advice that residents were given was that the ICAB would 
call them at some time and advise them of hotel arrangements for the night.  Some 
people were left waiting for a call that never came - they made their own arrangements 
deep into the early hours of the following morning. 
29.Moreover, those residents who had evacuated and had stayed in hotels, were advised to 
go to the homeless families unit the following morning.  This seems an unreasonable 
request in the circumstances.  One-third had made their own arrangements that night 
while two-thirds had been placed in hotels by the Council.  Some residents had left key 
personal belongings in their homes; such as glasses, contact lenses and hearing aids.  
Very few had an adequate range of clothing and footwear to last them a day, let alone 
oyster cards or whatever, to enable them to travel.  Only some had access to cash.   At 
that point it seemed that those Council’s tenants and leaseholders who fled fire in their 
8

homes had, in the immediate aftermath of the fire, to bend towards the bureaucracy of 
the Council rather than the bureaucracy bend to them.  
The following day - a corporate approach emerges 
30.Upon the conclusion of a corporate management meeting, acting as LA Gold, early the 
following day, officers from across the council went to the York Gardens library - which 
from that time acted as a community support centre - and swiftly established an array of 
personalised and targeted services for those impacted by the fire.   
31.Throughout that day, and the days that followed, a more organised and corporate 
response developed.  Excellent examples were identified where committed and capable 
staff teams found new ways of helping people to solve their practical problems.  The 
engagement of the local voluntary centre was also evident from that time.  The response 
of the data analytics team was referred to by many staff as enabling personalised 
responses to be developed swiftly.  This enabled a ‘caseworker’ approach to be devised 
so that each household was allocated a named worker.  It was impressive to hear how 
staff from across the Council were galvanised to contribute to helping the residents of 
Fox House solve their urgent and pressing practical challenges and some of their 
broader needs. 
32.It is conventional in emergency response to identify a ‘humanitarian’ strand of activity.  
Indeed, Councils are advised to established a humanitarian assistance liaison officer (a 
HALO), and usually for unitary Councils the authority’s Director of Adult Social Care.  
In this incident, the executive director performed the HALO role for the first week, and 
then the director for adult social care for the following two weeks. 
33.Humanitarian assistance often seems an awkward term to use in circumstances such as 
the Fox House fire.  People require highly practical and personalised assistance but they 
may also require a degree of pastoral care.  After all, people who lose their homes due to 
fire are experiencing circumstances of intense personal stress.  This pastoral or welfare 
care, could of course extend into specialist psychotherapeutic support, as well as other 
support that may be based on a ‘trauma informed’ approach to dealing with emergency 
situations.   
34.Immediately after an incident of this nature, people urgently require practical, financial 
and logistical assistance as well as empathic and compassionate support.  Balancing this 
can be difficult for Councils as they tend to approach people through a service 
functional lens.  Nonetheless, Wandsworth officers approached this well.  In 
Wandsworth, despite the perennial problem, that has been identified in many reviews, of 
allocating named caseworkers to residents, this aspect of the response worked 
reasonably well.  And weeks after the incident, officers are now engaged with a London 
wide review of key worker or caseworker roles in emergency situations - a review that is 
9

being led by Westminster City Council and is designed on the basis of experience gained 
in responding to the Grenfell tragedy.  
35.In the case of the Fox House fire, financial support was swiftly organised.  It took the 
form of subsistence vouchers of £25 per person per day, school uniform vouchers, 31 
laptops, 21 mobile phones, 16 basic furniture packages, and additional furniture 
packages.  There were some short term delays in relation to financial support (say, for 
purchasing new furniture).  This was especially in respect of those households who were 
uninsured - specifically in terms of their home contents and their urgent need for 
clothing, furniture and household goods.  This type of internal bureaucratic difficulty, is 
a long standing problem and its consideration follows a well known path.  Councils, like 
all other public authorities, need to have a prudent approach to allocating public money, 
and in so doing, they need to have regard to ‘moral hazard’ considerations.  But in these 
circumstances where the Council is the building owner with responsibility for the 
security and integrity of the building, such considerations can be otiose. In this instance, 
Wandsworth was flexible in its approach and created a fund for those in urgent need. 
36.Officers across the organisation have conducted a detailed internal review of what went 
right and wrong in respect of their ‘lessons learned’ work on the Fox House fire 
incident.  They have identified over 25 detailed points that arise from their learning 
lessons review - some practical, some organisational, some about improved 
communication channels, and others about being more foresighted.  This is a creditable 
piece of work that has been discussed corporately at the senior management level.  They 
have also identified how they can build on the effective corporate working that occurred 
in the days after the fire.   
37.Corporate working is more than better coordination and sharing information.  Corporate 
working in response to emergencies involves the development of coherent and 
integrated responses across the organisations many functional areas to bring comfort and 
stability to those individuals and communities impacted by the incident.  
38.However, in the Fox House fire incident, a swift housing response was also required: 
one in respect of rehousing those tenants and leaseholders who were displaced from 
their homes; and another in investigating the need for remedial works in other similar 
blocks in the locality - and possibly beyond. 
39.In very short order, the housing department identified alternative permanent 
accommodation for those who were displaced.  A new housing development was near to 
completion and enabled some households to be offered permanent accommodation very 
quickly.  Within three months (by 3rd December) 23 of the 29 Council tenants had been 
successfully rehoused; three had accepted offers and were due to move in shortly; and 
three others were had been made offers but were awaiting to view the properties. Of the 
five leaseholders, two moved through their own insurance arrangements, two have not 
approached the council for assistance, and one remains in a hotel as of early December.   
10

40.In respect of remedial works on the other similar or near identical blocks, this work has 
progressed quickly.  A ‘waking watch’ was initiated very speedily to offer vigilant 
assurance to tenants and leaseholders about alertness to any fire concerns.  And a 
specialist fire protection contractor has been engaged to assess the work that may be 
required on these other blocks.  A schedule of works proposed is already drafted.  This is 
a highly technical issue and requires the best professional expertise.  The LFB forensic 
investigation team has only recently been afforded access to the roof void at Fox House.  
This has, of course been subject to massive water penetration and a further degree of 
weathering.  And so the precise cause of the fire has yet to be determined.  However, the 
rapid spread of the fire may indicate a weakness of adequate compartmentation in the 
roof void at Fox House.   
41.The housing department have moved swiftly to address this issue.  They will need to 
work together with building control to ensure that the diagnostic inspections of roof 
voids (and potentially other communal voids in estate blocks), and they may need to 
engage relevant architectural or surveying expertise to direct and monitor, the 
contractors involved in rectifying any design or construction deficiencies.  I understand 
that this is precisely what they are engaged with at present.  And I applaud their speedy 
response.  They will need, of course, to keep residents, statutory partners and other 
relevant building owners appropriately aware of the success of these works as they 
progress.   
Member involvement 
42.The effective involvement of elected Members is a crucial aspect of successful Council 
responses to emergencies and civil contingencies.  Members know their local areas, they 
know many of the people in their areas - especially the informal community leaders and 
the local patchwork of civil associations that make up the fabric of their community.  As 
such Members offer a direct link to those most affected by the disaster.  But Members 
can wear four hats - a member of the political executive, a local ward member, a 
member of a scrutiny body, and a member of the body corporate - and each hat indicates 
the differing roles that Members can perform in respect of a Council’s response to an 
emergency. 
43.In November 2025, the Local Government Association (LGA) updated its guidance that 
is designed to support Councils in strengthening their approach to civil resilience. It 
provides an overview of the council’s responsibilities in this area, along with targeted 
sections outlining the role of senior councillors and ward councillors in the event of 
major incidents, emergencies or civil contingencies in their areas.  It is available here: 
https://www.local.gov.uk/publications/councillors-guide-civil-resilience. 
44.Members, in both boroughs, have had the opportunity of training and development (Jan 
2023, Richmond; and March 2023, Wandsworth) in how to operate in their roles in 
11

emergency situations - those in the political executive and those in their local ward 
community leadership role.  In this area, training involves understanding multi-agency 
roles and responsibilities in emergencies; the structure of executive management control 
and delegation (gold, silver, bronze, etc); and also what constitutes appropriate conduct 
in what can be distressing situations. This is now being refreshed, particularly in the 
light of updated guidance.    
The demands of being a first responder 
45.Too few Councils have seriously examined the demands of being a first responder in the 
event of a major emergency.  In 2004 when the Civil Contingencies Act was enacted, 
Councils tended to make incremental improvements in their emergency planning 
capability.  These were inadequate responses to the new legislation.  Instead the 
substantive challenge for Councils, is whether they have the capacity to respond, the 
capabilities of responding at scale, and the confidence to act alongside statutory partners 
as well as civil and community groups.   
46.Acting confidently and professionally is a substantive demand.  It requires expertise, 
local knowledge, a sense of public ethics in novel and uncertain situations, and a 
willingness to go beyond conventional boundaries to get things done.  Furthermore, 
emergencies and civil contingencies can test the legitimacy of public authorities, 
including Councils, in the court of public opinion.  In the case of very large incidents 
fundamental questions are often aired, such as, “Shouldn’t they have foreseen this?  
Have they responded properly and fairly”, and so on. One test is the ability to work 
collaboratively with the other first responders - fire, police and health services - as well 
as with local community and voluntary groups.  
47.In UK emergency management, the term ‘major incident’ is a multi-agency concept that 
derives from JESIP - the Joint Emergency Services Interoperability Principles.  These 
were created after major incident reviews (e.g. 7/7 in London) highlighted that the main 
failures were often in how agencies worked together, not in their individual technical 
capabilities.  The announcement of a ‘major incident’ typically features real or potential 
serious harm, large numbers of people or casualties, major disruption, and a need for 
exceptional coordination and command beyond normal business-as-usual and standard 
mutual aid. 
48.But Councils also need to respond to ‘critical’ and ‘significant’ incidents.  These are 
usually service-specific escalation levels within an organisation (the NHS, police or a 
local authority).  Who determines whether an incident is critical or significant is set in 
each organisation’s plan - it mostly relates to the degree or scale as to whether critical 
infrastructure or services are threatened.  Any responder agency can declare a major 
incident if the JESIP definition is met, whereas critical/significant levels are usually 
declared by designated managers under their internal policies.   
12

49.In the case of the Fox House fire, the LFB were the lead responder and would, in 
practice, be ‘in charge’ of the building until the fire was extinguished.  The Council’s 
building control function (in particular, the dangerous structure team) would be 
responsible for the building, and any subsequent access to it, in liaison with the housing 
department. 
50.The practicalities involved in organising a 24/7 coverage, at the management level, are 
easily under-estimated.  There are 365 days a year; which is 8,760 hours a year.  But 
people are usually employed on contracts that, depending upon their leave 
arrangements, requires them to work for 210 days - and an average of just 5% for 
sickness absence takes this down to 200.  And as each person will work for 8 hours for 
each of these 200 days - this produces 1,600 hours a year for each manager.  This means 
that for the equivalent of three people to be on duty the entire year, requires the Council 
to have at least 17 managers on a duty rota. 
51.In practice, duty rotas are particularly difficult to organise to ensure adequate overnight 
and weekend cover when managers are performing key functional roles on a day-to-day 
basis.  This means that when a ‘major incident’ happens, the key issue is how to ratchet 
up and scale up, the management and staffing response.  This is especially important in 
the period from 6pm during weekdays and over weekends.  It requires a change of 
mindset among those managers and staff who perform these vital response roles.  In 
emergencies everyone needs to have role clarity and be given clear tasks to perform and 
empowered to deliver for citizens - usually extending beyond their usual parameters, to 
ensure a coherent, complete and satisfactory response. 
Summary 
52.Overall the response of the Council’s organisation to the Fox House fire can be 
described in outline by four parts.  First, a highly demanding set of challenges that 
began at 7pm with the rapid spread of the fire, was addressed by excellent work on the 
part of staff and councillors.  With dedication, they worked hard into the night, 
extending into early the next morning.  But, as there often is in the first few hours of an 
emergency, there were practical, logistical and communication missteps that hampered 
the first few hours of their aspect of the multi-agency response.  Things could have been 
organised more smoothly and satisfactorily for those residents who were evacuated and 
who needed urgent overnight accommodation.    
53.Second, the Council’s organisational response improved dramatically from the following 
morning.  Corporate working was evident and staff from across the Council’s functional 
areas stepped forward to help those most affected by the incident. Working from the 
community support centre and across the authority. 
54.Third, the response of the housing department to rehousing people has been excellent.  
In very short order, the housing department identified alternative accommodation for 
13

those who were displaced.  Just three months after the Fox House fire a total of 23 
households, out of 29 Council tenants, have accepted the offer of a permanent 
alternative home; three have accepted properties and are planning to move; while three 
others have yet to view and accept their property offers.  Of course, the five leaseholders 
have had to grapple with many issues directly themselves - one experiencing great 
difficulties gaining financial support from the relevant insurance companies.  This latter 
case is (in the second week of December) in the process of being resolved by the 
Council’s financial services team.  In respect of rehousing tenants, credit must also go to 
the leading members in the political executive who gave clear direction to officers and 
who promptly agreed the proposals to rehouse the displaced residents of the Fox House 
fire as a Council priority. 
55.Fourth, the housing department was quick to recognise the potentially wider problem 
that the Fox House fire alerted them to.  A ‘waking watch’ was initiated very speedily to 
offer vigilant assurance to tenants and leaseholders in neighbouring blocks.  And a 
specialist fire protection contractor has been engaged to assess the work that may be 
required on these other blocks.  This is a highly technical issue and requires the best 
professional expertise.  The housing department have moved swiftly to address this 
issue.  They will need, of course, to keep residents, statutory partners and other relevant 
building owners appropriately aware of the success of these works as they progress.   
Recommendations 
Organisational leadership and Gold/Silver/Bronze 
1.Strengthen and formalise the Gold, Silver and Bronze arrangements, including a clear, 
sustainable duty rota of senior managers, so that the Council can scale up rapidly and 
confidently when incidents occur. Adopt the appropriate involvement of Members of the 
political executive, local ward councillors and those exercising a scrutiny role in the 
governance and oversight of relevant decisions and actions. 
2.In any incident that meets the JESIP definition of a major incident, ensure that a senior 
director with delegated authority to commit resources is physically on site within two 
hours of the incident being declared, in close liaison with the Bronze/LALO and with 
clear lines of communication to Gold. 
3.Make explicit, and routinely brief on, the protocols for decision-making, escalation and 
governance (including the roles of the political executive, scrutiny, and corporate 
management) in emergency situations. 
Operational readiness and systems 
4.Review “grab bag’ arrangements for the essential materials needed by onsite staff 
(phones, chargers, preset household identification reports, etc).  Recognise the reality of 
ubiquitous, real-time and viral social media, and ensure that formal communication 
14

channels are continuous, dynamic and well-coordinated, providing clear, calm and 
accurate information to affected residents, councillors, partners and the wider public. 
5.Ensure that control of all key contractors and commissioned providers are visible to 
on-site management as well as to those operating in Gold, Silver, Bronze and LALO 
roles, with the ability to escalate or switch providers quickly if needed. 
6.Review the current arrangements with ICAB and test whether they can reliably meet the 
time-critical demands of large-scale evacuations; if not, explore more robust and 
scalable arrangements, potentially on a pan-London or sub-regional basis, with other 
councils and housing providers for emergency hotel and serviced accommodation. 
Residents, humanitarian support and members 
7.Embed a consistently corporate and humanitarian approach in all incidents, ensuring that 
processes, financial controls and eligibility rules can flex in the immediate aftermath so 
that residents are not required to “fit” standard service pathways when they have just lost 
access to their homes and possessions. 
8.Train and develop managers and staff to operate in fully corporate responses, including 
joint training and exercising with statutory partners and voluntary sector organisations, 
so that role clarity, delegated authority and expectations are understood before an 
incident occurs. 
9.Provide dedicated training and guidance for councillors, in line with the updated LGA 
councillors’ guide to civil resilience, covering their roles as ward members, executive 
members, scrutineers and corporate guardians, and how they should operate safely and 
constructively in emergency situations. 
Technical learning and building safety 
10.Seek further expert advice on both the cause of the fire and the rapid spread within the 
roof space, and on the full range of feasible remedial and rectification works across Fox 
House and similar blocks, drawing on specialist fire engineering, architectural and 
surveying expertise. 
11.Work jointly across housing, building control and relevant external experts to design and 
deliver a programme of diagnostic inspections of roof voids and other communal voids, 
and to oversee any remedial works required, ensuring that residents and statutory 
partners are kept appropriately informed as this programme progresses. 
12.When appropriate, issue notifications and share learning with other building owners, 
landlords and professional bodies so that, when the full facts are known, the lessons 
from the Fox House fire inform wider practice on compartmentation, roof construction 
and fire spread in similar post-war blocks.
