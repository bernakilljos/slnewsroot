1. Executive Summary  
 
1.1. Following the inspection of housing services a detailed action plan has 
been developed which addresses the immediate areas for improvement 
identified by the Regulator and the longer-term transformation of 
housing into a high-quality service, better able to meet the needs of its 
tenants. 
 
1.2. The immediate actions were agreed with the Regulator, with progress 
monitored via monthly meetings. Since the Regulatory Judgement was 
published in February: 
 
 A rolling programme of stock condition surveys has commenced 
and since May 2025, 3,392 surveys have been completed. 
 Considerable progress has been made with the programme of 
electrical safety tests. Approximately 87% of homes and 94% of 
communal spaces have a satisfactory Electrical Installation 
Condition Report. 
 Outstanding remedial actions from Fire Risk Assessments has 
reduced from 1,800 at the time of inspection to 326. 
 The number of overdue repairs has decreased from 2,800 to 1,500. 
 
1.3. Extra capacity has been focussed on repairs with the Council’s Delivery 
Unit undertaking a review of the repairs process.  
 
1.4. It was also recognised that as well as procedural changes, a cultural 
change was required to transform the way services are delivered to

Responding to the Regulatory Judgement  
 
2.3. Over the past ten years the requirements of so cial housing providers 
have changed significantly, primarily being driven by the Grenfell Tower 
fire in 2017. All social housing providers are having to adjust their 
service models to address new legislative environments which are:  
 
 Increased powers of the Regulator of Social Housin g and the 
Housing Ombudsman, and the introduction of the Building Safety 
Regulator. 
 Greater emphasis on health and safety responsibili ties of social 
landlords. 
 Greater emphasis on having accurate and timely dat a in relation 
to managed stock. 
 Introduction of new legislation through Awaab’s La w relating to 
the management and treatment of damp and mould. 
 An emerging requirement to deliver a reduction in carbon 
emissions which will involve significant retrofit programmes and is 
being combined into a new Decent Homes Standard. 
 
2.4. In February 2025, the Regulator of Social Hous ing issued Wandsworth 
with a C3 regulatory judgement [see Paper No. 25-133]. This 
judgement reflects serious failings in delivering the consumer standards 
introduced by the Regulator in April 2023. The principal areas found for 
improvement relate to the Safety and Quality Standard, specifically 
noted by the Regulator were: 
 
 A lack of up-to-date information on the condition of homes 
managed. 
 Incomplete programme of electrical safety checks. 
 1,800 fire remedial actions overdue. 
 
2.5. The Regulator also recommended improvements in  the Transparency, 
Influence and Accountability Standard.  While it was noted the Council 
had a well-established formal framework for engagement with tenants, 
there was a need to provide further informal routes and improve tenant-
led engagement. It is both the Safety and Quality and Transparency,

Council’s work throughout 2025.

but also Transparency, Influence and Accountability.

3. Future challenges   
 
3.1. For context, it is important to recognise that  in the post-Grenfell space 
the regulatory requirement of social landlords and its duties have 
expanded significantly. This was set out in the 58 recommendations 
arising from the Grenfell Inquiry. These placed expanded statutory 
duties, higher compliance standards and greater accountability on 
landlords. 
 
3.2. Most social landlords began responding to this  changing environment 
after the Social Housing White Paper (2020) by investing in and 
enhancing their housing and building safety teams, investing in 
competence and systems and embedding resident engagement at the 
core of service delivery. 
 
3.3. Wandsworth did not significantly change its pr actices following the 
White Paper. This failure to adapt meant that Wandsworth was not 
managing its stock in compliance with the consumer standards set by 
the Regulator of Social Housing, particularly when it came to stock 
condition data. Consequently, when the inspection started in November 
2024 and completed in February 2025, the services were found non-
compliant and resulted in regulatory intervention and reputational 
damage.  
 
3.4. The HIT Plan sets out actions that must be del ivered over the next 36 
months to move the Council to a position of compliance which will 
ensure it provides safe homes for its tenants. 
 
3.5. The start of that process will focus the Counc il’s attention in 2026/27 on 
the following specific areas: 
 
Understanding stock data and condition 
 

 
 
Official 
 
 
3.6. The Council has now committed to an accelerate d programme of stock 
condition surveys to tenanted homes to achieve 100% by December 
2026.  This work is progressing well with initial targets being surpassed 
which provides confidence that the 100% surveys will be completed on 
time.   These surveys include an assessment using the Housing Health 
and Safety Rating System (HHSRS).  Surveys will ensure we have a 
full understanding of the condition of our homes, can react quickly to 
any safety concerns and can add necessary works into the capital 
programme. From the 1 
st  April 2027, we will commence a five-year re-
survey programme with 20% of stock being surveyed each year to 
ensure information remains fit for purpose for investment. 
 
3.7.  
 
Improving the repairs service 
 
3.8. Tenants and leaseholders have told us that the  repairs service needs to 
improve. Whilst not recognised as an area of serious failing by the 
Regulator, we are committed to responding to this and are prioritising 
sustained changes to the service. This will involve redesigning the 
service, increasing the use of digital platforms used to log repairs and 
simplifying processes. Satisfaction levels with the repairs service 
remain low (58%) when compared to similar landlords in London (65%). 
Since August, officers from Housing have been collaborating closely 
with the Council’s Delivery Unit to better understand where challenges 
are, including processes for reporting and undertaking repairs.  
 
Understanding vulnerable households 
 
3.9. Considerable progress has been made since the publication of the 
Vulnerable Households Policy in November 2024. Building on this 
foundation, a comprehensive audit of data on vulnerable households is 
scheduled for 2026. This audit will strengthen our understanding of 
residents’ needs and support the development of Personal Emergency 
Evacuation Plans for those living in high-rise buildings. It will also 
enable more tailored engagement and service delivery, ensuring that 

 
 
Official 
support is responsive and effective.  
 
Increasing participation and engagement  
 
3.10. Strong progress has been achieved in strength ening the ‘tenant voice’, 
and further enhancements are underway. In their inspection, the 
Regulator emphasised the need for more opportunities for tenant-led 
engagement. The Council is responding proactively. The Participation 
Register has been significantly expanded and now includes over 700 
residents (of which, 500 are tenants), demonstrating growing interest 
and involvement. A comprehensive engagement calendar for 2026 is in 
development and will be refreshed throughout the year to maintain 
momentum. In addition, an audit of the Council’s resident participation 
structures will be commissioned early in the new year to ensure these 
arrangements remain effective and inclusive.  
 
Achieving health and safety compliance 
 
3.11. To ensure the Council meets its statutory hea lth and safety obligations 
and provides assurance to its tenants and the Regulator, we have 
undertaken a desktop assessment of existing staffing and budget 
resources and identified two key points. 
 
3.12. Firstly, the existing compliance staff are sh ared across the width of the 
Housing Management service, creating a disjointed service model 
which is not coordinating its abilities or resources to deliver existing 
services. 
 
3.13. Secondly, there are additional responsibiliti es arising from the Building 
Safety Act (2022) which has a particular focus on the management and 
maintenance of high-rise buildings. The Council has 148 high-rise 
buildings, and needs to increase its staffing capacity to undertake the 
work. 
 
3.14. By establishing and investing in a dedicated team, the Council will be 
better resourced to ensure ongoing compliance across its housing 
stock, mitigate risk, and avoid the financial and reputational 
consequences of non-compliance. 
 
3.15. This will provide further assurance to both t he Regulator of Social 
Housing and the Building Safety Regulator, ensuring that the Safety 

 
 
Official 
and Quality standard is fully met. It should be noted that this team will 
be managing an annual budget of approximately £16 million excluding 
major capital programmes (which they will also lead). 
 
3.16. A single team will be more efficient and effe ctive and will provide better 
value for money through the avoidance of duplication. Once embedded, 
this will positively impact the activities of frontline housing management 
staff, enabling improvements in service as well as efficiencies in 
operational staffing levels. 
 
3.17. For tenants, this means greater assurance tha t homes are safe, well-
maintained and compliant with all building safety standards. It will also 
lead to improved communications and swifter resolutions, with accurate 
stock data being acted on in a timely manner.

4.1. This improvement plan marks the end of the old  era and the beginning 
of new chapter in how Wandsworth operates as a social landlord. 
Bringing our responsibilities and systems as a housing provider up to 
the modern standards that our tenants expect and deserve.  
 
4.2. For decades the previous administration champi oned the ‘Wandsworth 
way’, but what we have found is that many of the corporate systems 
and models that we have inherited were not fit for the new regulatory 
environment and standards that social housing providers are expected 
to meet.  
 
4.3. For years we were told how impressive the Coun cil’s 100% decent 
homes standard was, but what good is a 100% decent homes rating if it 
is based on a stock condition survey of only 1% of our homes? This 
plan will help us understand our stock fully, what work needs to be 
done, and help us prepare for the future. 
 
4.4. This improvement plan will put us on the road to delivering the best 
service for our tenants and crucially putting their voices and experience 
at the centre of our decision making.

Executive summary 
In response to receiving a C3 regulatory judgement, Wandsworth Council undertook 
a comprehensive Root Cause Analysis (RCA) to identify the underlying factors 
contributing to service failings within its housing services. This reflective exercise, led 
by senior management and supported by an external consultant, aimed to ensure 
that robust mitigations are in place to prevent recurrence and to support both 
regulatory compliance and long-term service transformation. 
The RCA identified four key themes: the position of the department (and its 
relationship with the wider organisation), departmental culture, data and systems, 
and performance management. Historically, the Housing department operated with 
limited corporate oversight, leading to siloed practices and insufficient scrutiny of 
critical areas such as stock condition and building safety. Cultural challenges, 
including slow responsiveness to change and a reluctance to act on benchmarking, 
further hindered service improvement.  A performance culture was not pervasive 
across the department, despite evidence of good practice within some sections. 
To address these issues, the Council has implemented a new governance 
framework, restructured its housing services into a broader Resident Services 
directorate, and initiated a full review of the repairs process. Efforts are also 
underway to improve data quality and accessibility through system integration and 
clearer data ownership. A renewed focus on performance management, resident 
engagement, and contract oversight is central to driving sustainable improvements. 
This RCA has directly informed the Housing Improvement and Transformation Plan 
and is a key step in strengthening the Council’s ability to deliver safe, responsive, 
and resident-focused housing services. Enhanced governance and oversight, 
cultural change and a commitment to continuous improvement will ensure that 
Wandsworth is better positioned to meet regulatory standards and the expectations 
of its residents. 
 
Why Wandsworth has undertaken a Root Cause Analysis (RCA) 
Wandsworth Council undertook an RCA to identify the reasons for failings in its most 
recent regulatory inspection and to implement measures that not only address these 
issues but meet our broader ambitions of making Wandsworth a fairer, 
compassionate and more sustainable borough.   
This exercise was led by senior management with input and fresh ideas being 
provided from an external consultant.  The RCA identified four themes, each of these 
consisting of different challenges. These are: 
 Position of Housing 
 Departmental culture 
 Data and systems 
 Performance

Mitigations have been identified and agreed.  These include short-, medium- and 
long-term measures to ensure continuous improvements to services that residents

A Housing Improvement and Transformation Plan was developed in partnership with

Senior managers reviewed a broad range of data and insights and whilst the

Position of Housing 
The previous Housing Department encompassed Housing Management and 
Housing Services sections, Strategy, Compliance and Enabling and Regeneration 
sections.  This included over 600 staff members responsible for providing services 
for over 33,000 tenants and leaseholders.  The Housing Department historically 
operated at a distance from corporate oversight, resulting in limited strategic 
accountability and siloed working.  This has led to gaps in understanding among 
senior leaders and elected members, particularly around critical issues such as stock 
condition.   
Whilst scrutiny of housing delivery has increased since 2024, particularly considering 
Wandsworth's Tenant Satisfaction Measure performance, there remains greater 
opportunity for scrutiny from senior managers with senior (named) officers 
accountable for delivery against their respective areas.  For example, in relation to 
electrical testing and other health and safety measures, these areas did not receive 
a sufficient level of scrutiny from outside the department, although this data is now 
reported as a quarterly corporate measure. 
Since the inspection, a new governance framework has been established.  This 
includes oversight from the Chief Executive and Senior Leadership Team, a 
dedicated Housing Improvement Programme Board, chaired by the Executive 
Director of Resident Services, and an expanded range of performance measures 
shared with senior leaders and councillors.  The newly agreed measures reflect the 
actions required to provide the Regulator of Social Housing with assurance that it is 
delivering each of the four consumer standards.  They also incorporate longer-term 
commitments that are included within the Housing Improvement and Transformation 
Plan. 
Member oversight of the department is also supported by a dedicated Task and 
Finish group.  The group has cross-party membership and convenes monthly to 
review performance, service delivery and key issues for thematic areas (for example, 
repairs improvements, stock condition and more).  The meetings are also attended 
by a range of senior officers.  There is also a detailed, annual presentation of

It was also acknowledged that an internal departmental focus limited the opportunity 
to learn from good practice from other services.  To address this, the Council has

increase.  In addition, it is an opportunity for those services that are performing 
strongly and helping achieve high levels of satisfaction to showcase their work.  
Equally, this is an opportunity to build on collaboration beyond housing.   
 
Departmental Culture 
Departmental culture and practices were identified as areas requiring change to 
support long-term service improvement.  Some entrenched behaviours and structural 
issues have limited the department’s ability to adapt, innovate, and respond 
effectively to resident needs and regulatory expectations.  Where previous 
recommendations or plans of action have been prepared, changes were not 
implemented swiftly.  By encouraging behavioural change, housing teams will be 
better placed to meet the diverse needs of residents. 
For example, this meant that in some areas, processes remained in place that other 
landlords had since moved away from.  This led to established ways of working that 
were not necessarily achieving high quality outputs for residents.  Whilst 
benchmarking was regularly undertaken to better understand best practice within the 
sector, findings were not always acted upon.  For example, in the areas of recording 
repairs and creating opportunities for resident engagement, traditional models 
remained in place.   
It is important to note that the Council was a founder member of the Association of 
Retained Council Housing (ARCH) and regularly participated in London Councils 
meetings. We are strengthening this experience by learning from other councils, for 
example, this has included engaging with other local authorities, specifically Camden 
and Enfield.  Acting on learning is crucial to the ongoing development of the service.  
A full review of the repairs process is underway, supported by the Council’s internal 
Delivery Unit and external consultants Roretti.  The repairs review is informed by 
resident insight from focus groups as well as comparative performance data and 
learning from other providers.  The review will conclude in December 2025 and aims 
to transform the resident repairs journey. 
A reactive approach to building safety prioritising short-term fixes over strategic long-
term investment was also identified. To address this, the Housing Improvement 
Programme Board is looking at structures for a more robust and better resourced

Building Safety team to ensure a long-term, risk-based approach to safety and 
compliance in the management of our stock.  
Customer focus has been highlighted as an issue when delivering services. This 
stems from increasing demands being placed on frontline-staff, such as Estate 
Managers, without a sufficiently detailed review by senior management of their 
capacity to fulfil these roles effectively with the increased workload. Consequently, 
frontline officers have become more reactive than proactive in addressing residents’ 
needs, leading to a diminished focus on customer service which has impacted 
residents’ perception of the Council’s management of its housing stock. 
Efforts are being undertaken to encourage a greater customer focus by designing 
services around the diverse needs of residents.   This is being tackled through 
increased resident involvement in service design and scrutiny, supported by better 
use of data and insight and making important reasonable adjustments for those who 
require them.  The focus groups held in March and April 2025 gave useful insight into 
experiences of those who were dissatisfied with repairs or complaints. 
Other cultural challenges include slow responses to legislative change and a 
tendency to rely on operational experience rather than strategic planning. These are 
being addressed through improved governance, broader engagement with staff and 
members and a shift towards co-produced services that reflect the lived experiences 
of residents. 
The Council acknowledged that decision-making was centralised; limited to a core 
team of directors.  This is being addressed through the Decision Making and 
Accountability programme which will ultimately create a flatter management structure 
with fewer hierarchies.  Equally, the move to Resident Services has created a 
broader leadership group.  There will be greater opportunities for staff to identify or 
suggest areas for improvement, through increased engagement and by promoting 
psychological safety within teams.  This will be complemented by increased 
collaboration across the organisation.   
 
Data and systems 
As a large department encompassing many services, different teams have 
developed their own methods of recording and maintaining data.  This presents 
challenges when trying to source key data for improving services or monitoring 
delivery.  Whilst tenancy and property information is held on NEC and fire safety 
functions (e.g. front door inspections and fire risk assessments) are on RiskBase, a 
number of services were found to be using spreadsheets for the storing of data.  This 
presents a challenge when trying to access data in a timely manner particularly if 
there are limited data owners involved.  To ameliorate this, the Council is developing 
a single source of truth using its NEC and RiskBase platforms. This will involve a 
data mapping exercise to clearly identify data owners – service leads ultimately 
responsible for signing off data and performance information at a local level.  This 
will be complemented by ongoing campaigns to encourage staff engagement with

Oﬃcial 
the applications.  Many services manage data by using dashboards.  These will give 
a real-time view on the current performance and challenges 
The key risk associated with data inconsistencies is not having important and up to 
date information on the safety and condition of tenants’ homes.  Whilst the Council 
was confident that its approach to data management did not compromise the safety 
of residents, it was agreed that accessing information in a timely manner was 
challenging.  By moving towards universal - rather than local - systems, data 
harmonisation will make it easier for detailed analysis to be undertaken and, 
subsequently, for this to inform key decisions.  A Task and Finish Group has recently 
been established and will lead on overseeing this work, including, improving the 
quality of datasets used.  
It was also evident that functionality of the NEC system could be utilised further.  
There is capability for additional resources to be used which could aid the repairs 
process and increase overall efficiency.  These will be explored in more detail 
through the repairs review that is currently taking place.  Officers involved in the 
repairs review have already liaised with other local authorities to identify how they 
are using their systems for recording repairs. 
Data and insight are now being used in a series of meetings with senior leaders, 
monthly meetings with the Chief Executive and Senior Leadership Team have been 
established.  In addition, there is regular engagement with the Cabinet Member for 
Housing and representatives from the Regulator of Social Housing. These steps aim 
to increase scrutiny, accountability, and early identification of risks ensuring that 
decisions are taken making the best use of data and insight. 
 
Performance 
Performance information is at the heart of shaping and improving services.  For 
example, where satisfaction levels are low or services underperform against sectoral 
benchmarks, action is taken.  This has led to improvements in key areas such as 
complaints handling and repairs.  This analysis however has identified that a 
performance culture should be embedded across all teams.  Consequently, it is 
expected that negative trends or underperformance can be acted upon more swiftly.  
Addressing worsening performance, in the relevant forums, with senior officers is a 
key component to driving improvements.  Greater ownership of performance was 
also identified as a challenge in some areas of the department.  Residents have a 
right to know how well their services are being provided and how contracts are being 
managed. 
Since the inspection, there has been an increased use of performance data to inform 
service delivery.  As mentioned earlier in this report, new governance arrangements 
are in place, with performance data a central component of these discussions.  The 
improvement plan has lead officers (or owners) named, increasing accountability.  
Performance information includes risks and finance information, whereby if a 
budgetary pressure is identified, it can be acted upon.  These changes will make it 
easier for failures to be addressed and a requisite level of support provided.

Conclusion 
The RCA has identified areas which contributed towards the Regulator’s C3 rating

put Wandsworth in a stronger position ahead of a future inspection.  The analysis

governance in place, it is expected that changes to practices and greater oversight of 
delivery, the Council can deliver an improved housing service, better positioned to 
meet the needs of residents.
